Research

My research can be thought of as around two general topics. First, I am interested in the metaphysics of "extraordinary" objects (as opposed to ordinary objects), such as Dao (as opposed to the myriad things), God (as opposed to creatures), stuff (as opposed to individuals), form/matter (as opposed to objects that are constituted by forms and matter), and propositions (as opposed to things that are represented by propositions). Second, I am interested in the metaphysics of representation, especially the question "What makes something represent or represented?" (or, is the "representing vs. being represented" distinction well grounded at all?) Accordingly, I work on topics of grounding, metaphysical indeterminacy, the ontology of stuff, the nature of absence and nothingness, and those that lie at the intersection of metaphysics and philosophy of language such as truthmaking, the nature of propositions, and ineffability.

My research is topic or question based. I try to engage with ideas and theories as long as they are relevant to the topics I am interested in, no matter which philosophical traditions they are from. While I am reluctant to call myself an expert in any philosophical tradition, my works interact with resources in contemporary analytic metaphysics and metametaphysics, Aristotelian and Thomistic metaphysics, Neoplatonism, early modern rationalism, Daoism, Neo-daoism, Neo-confucianism, and Buddhism. I also believe that it is impossible to exhaust theoretical resources on any topic in philosophy, and it is normal that we only focus on resources in a limited range of philosophical traditions. For example, I am more familiar with contemporary analytic philosophy and Chinese philosophy than other traditions. I take "what you say has already been said by X in the tradition of Y" to be an appraise rather than a criticism.

Recently, I have been thinking a lot about a group of "high-order" relations that are intuitively similar but carry different ontological implications, including the determinable/determinate relation, the type/token relation, the genus/species relation, the universal/trope relation, the instantiation relation, the part/whole relation, the set formation, the schema/instance relation, the generalization/instantiation relation, among others.
